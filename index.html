Lorenzo Palazzetti

Computer Scientist, HALY.ID Fellow at the University of Perugia.  |  lorenzo.palazzetti@collaboratori.unipg.it

He received the Bachelor and Master degrees in Computer Science from the University of Perugia, Italy, in 2018 and 2020, respectively. He earned a PhD in Computer Science with praise at the University of Florence, Italy, in 2024. He is currently a HALY.ID Fellow at Univerisity of Perugia. His research interests include design and analysis of algorithms, combinatorial optimization, unmanned vehicles, computer vision.

Education

2020-2023

University of Florence

PhD Student

Judgment:
excellent with praise

Lorenzo explored and optimized drone-based solutions for last-mile delivery and smart agriculture. He devised novel strategies for complex combinatorial problems in route optimization and agricultural operations. Additionally, Lorenzo investigated computer vision techniques for pest monitoring in orchards using drones, leading to an autonomous protocol for pest management. His dissertation, "Optimizing Drone-based Applications for Delivery and Smart Agriculture," reflects his contributions to the field. Lorenzo was honored with a PhD with distinction for his significant contributions.

2018-2020

University of Perugia

Master of Science

Judgment:
Summa cum laude

Lorenzo's master's studies covers deeply algorithms, data structures, theoretical computer science, operative research, and artificial intelligence. His thesis, "Routing Drones Being Aware of Wind Conditions," explored the influence of wind on drone routes, particularly in last-mile delivery contexts. He revealed the substantial impact of wind on drone flight paths and optimized routes using various algorithms. Synthetic simulations were employed to conduct feasibility analyses leveraging on those algorithms.

2015-2018

University of Perugia

Bachelor of Science

Judgment:
104/110

During his undergraduate studies, Lorenzo delved into the core principles of computer science, focusing on algorithms, data structures, networking, and programming. For his thesis titled "Lattice-Based Cryptography," Lorenzo embarked on an exploration of cryptographic systems robust against Quantum Computers. Inspired by NTRU, he developed a cryptosystem designed to withstand quantum attacks.
